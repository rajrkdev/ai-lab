Word Embedding vs Sentence Embedding

From Word2Vec to BERT — Understanding How Text Becomes Meaning

The Big Picture

Embeddings convert text into vectors — sequences of numbers that represent meaning in a mathematical space. The key insight: meaning becomes measurable. Similar concepts produce vectors that are close together, and you can do arithmetic on ideas.

Think of embeddings like GPS coordinates for meaning. Just as two locations near each other on a map are physically close, two words or sentences with similar meaning have vectors that are close together in embedding space.

Word Embedding

Maps each individual word to a fixed vector. Fast and simple, but cannot capture context. "bank" always gets the same vector regardless of whether it's a river bank or a financial bank.

Word2VecGloVeFastText

Sentence Embedding

Maps an entire sentence or paragraph to a single vector. Uses transformer attention to capture context — "bank" near "river" becomes a completely different vector than "bank" near "money".

What is a Vector?

A vector is a list of numbers. Each number encodes how strongly the text relates to some learned abstract concept. A simplified 3-dimensional word vector might look like:

king  = [0.85,  0.92,  0.10]
queen = [0.88,  0.11,  0.09]
man   = [0.12,  0.90,  0.15]
        ↑       ↑      ↑
      royalty   male  youth  (conceptual — not actual labels)

In practice, these vectors have 300–1536 dimensions — each corresponding to an abstract learned feature (not labeled by humans). The model learns these features automatically from billions of text examples.

Why does this matter? Once text is a vector, you can measure similarity with simple math (cosine similarity), cluster documents without labels, find nearest neighbors at scale, and power semantic search in RAG systems.

Word Embedding

Word embeddings map individual words to vectors. The most famous technique is Word2Vec (2013, Google). It learns by asking one question repeatedly across billions of sentences:

"Given the word 'king', what words tend to appear nearby in text?"

How Word2Vec Trains

Word2Vec slides a window over text. Two training modes:

CBOW Mode

Continuous Bag of Words
Given: [the, ___, sat, on] → Predict: "cat"

Faster training. Good for frequent words.

Skip-gram Mode

Given: "cat" → Predict: [the, sat, on, mat]

Slower but better for rare words.

The Famous Analogy

Directions in vector space encode semantic relationships:

king - man + woman ≈ queen
Paris - France + Italy ≈ Rome
walking - walk + swim ≈ swimming

This works because gender, royalty, and country-capital each become consistent directions across the learned space.

The Critical Limitation: No Context

Word2Vec assigns ONE fixed vector per word — regardless of context. This completely breaks for polysemous words:

The "bank" problem

SentenceWord2Vec vector for "bank"Correct?
"I went to the river bank"[0.2, 0.9, 0.1, ...]Same vector!
"I deposited money at the bank"[0.2, 0.9, 0.1, ...]Same vector!
Word2Vec cannot tell these sentences apart. Context is completely lost — the model doesn't know whether "bank" means nature or finance.

Other Word Embedding Models

GloVe (Stanford, 2014)

Uses global co-occurrence statistics across the whole corpus (not just local windows). Faster training, comparable quality.

FastText (Facebook, 2016)

Breaks words into character n-grams.
"running" = "run" + "runn" + "unni"
Handles unknown words and typos. Major advantage over Word2Vec.

Sentence Embedding

Sentence embeddings fix the context problem. Instead of one vector per word, you get one vector for the entire input — capturing full meaning, word relationships, and context.

The Key Innovation: Self-Attention

Modern sentence embedders (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, voyage-large-2) are built on BERT-style transformers. The core mechanism is self-attention:

In the sentence "I went to the river bank", the word "bank" strongly attends to "river". This high attention weight shifts bank's vector toward a nature/geography meaning — completely different from "bank" in "deposited money at the bank".

Step-by-Step: How Sentence Embedding Works

Step 1
Tokenise
Split text into subword tokens: "unhappiness" → ["un", "##happi", "##ness"]
Step 2
Initial Token Embeddings
Each token gets an initial static vector + positional encoding (tells the model where in the sentence the token appears)
Step 3
Attention Layers (×6 to ×24)
Every token attends to every other token. Line thickness = attention weight. "bank" looks at "river" with high weight → context shifts its representation.
Step 4
Contextual Token Vectors
Each token now has a context-aware vector. "bank" in a river sentence has a completely different vector than "bank" in a finance sentence.
Step 5
Mean Pooling → One Sentence Vector
Average all token vectors into a single fixed-size vector. This is the sentence embedding. One number per dimension, representing the whole sentence's meaning.

Why Mean Pooling?

CLS token pooling  → Use only the first [CLS] token's vector
Mean pooling       → Average ALL token vectors  ← better for similarity
Max pooling        → Take max value per dimension

Mean pooling (used by SBERT) captures information from every token equally and has shown better performance on semantic similarity benchmarks than CLS pooling.

Bi-Encoder vs Cross-Encoder

In retrieval systems, two different architectures serve different stages:

Bi-Encoder

Stage 1: Retrieval

Embeds query and document separately, then compares with cosine similarity.

Fast. Scales to millions of docs. Powers vector database search.

Cross-Encoder (Reranker)

Stage 2: Reranking

Feeds query + document together through the model. Much more accurate.

Slow. Used only on top-20 to 100 results from bi-encoder retrieval.

Static vs Dynamic Embeddings

This is the most important conceptual shift in the field:

Static (Word2Vec era)

One fixed vector per vocabulary word. "Bank" is always the same point in space — doesn't matter what surrounds it.

vocab["bank"] = [0.2, 0.9, 0.1, ...]
# Same regardless of context

Dynamic / Contextual (BERT era)

Vector for each token changes based on surrounding context. "Bank" in a nature sentence gets a completely different vector than "bank" in finance.

encode("river bank")  → [0.1, 0.3, 0.8]
encode("money bank")  → [0.7, 0.1, 0.2]

Cosine Similarity

The standard way to measure how similar two embeddings are. It measures the angle between two vectors — not their magnitude:

cosine_similarity(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)
Result range: -1 (opposite) → 0 (unrelated) → +1 (identical meaning)
Cosine similarity is preferred over Euclidean distance because two sentences can have the same meaning but different vector magnitudes (e.g., longer sentences tend to have larger magnitude). Cosine similarity normalises this away.

Evolution of Text Embeddings

2013
Word2Vec (Google)
Static word vectors via neural word prediction. Introduced the concept of word arithmetic: king - man + woman ≈ queen.
2014
GloVe (Stanford)
Global co-occurrence statistics. Combines count-based and predictive approaches. Faster training, comparable quality.
2016
FastText (Facebook)
Subword character n-grams. "running" = "run" + "runn" + "unni". Handles unknown words and typos — a major advantage over Word2Vec.
2018
ELMo (AllenAI)
First context-dependent word vectors, from a bidirectional LSTM. Proved that dynamic embeddings work better for downstream tasks.
2018
BERT (Google)
Bidirectional transformer encoder. Masked language modelling and next sentence prediction. Became the foundation for virtually all modern NLP.
2019
SBERT (Sentence-BERT)
Fine-tuned BERT for semantic similarity using contrastive loss on NLI datasets. Made sentence similarity practical at scale with bi-encoder architecture.
2021
all-MiniLM-L6-v2
Distilled SBERT model. 384 dimensions, 6 layers, extremely fast. Became the default embedding model for lightweight RAG systems.
2023+
voyage-large-2, text-embedding-3-large
Domain-optimised retrieval embeddings. Higher quality at 1024–1536 dims, trained specifically for retrieval tasks. Outperform general-purpose models on domain-specific corpora.
