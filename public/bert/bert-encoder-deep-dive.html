Residual connections — why they are critical

The + x after every sub-layer is arguably the most important engineering decision in the Transformer. Without it, BERT could not be trained at 12 layers.
The vanishing gradient problem
Without residuals, gradients must flow through every multiplication in every layer. Each weight matrix multiplies the gradient by values typically < 1. After 12 layers: 0.9¹² = 0.28. After 24 layers (BERT-Large): 0.9²⁴ = 0.08. Lower layers receive almost no learning signal.

With residuals, the gradient has a direct highway back to early layers: ∂L/∂x has an additive term of 1 (identity) that never shrinks.
Layer Normalization
Applied after every residual addition:

LayerNorm(x) = γ × (x − μ) / (σ + ε) + β

γ and β are learned scale + shift (768 params each). μ and σ computed over the 768 features for each token independently. Keeps activations in a stable range, preventing exploding gradients and reducing sensitivity to weight initialisation.

Feed-Forward Network — the "thinking" layer

The FFN runs after self-attention in every block. It applies the same transformation independently to each token.

Why 4× expansion?
The hidden dimension of 3072 (4 × 768) controls the FFN's "storage capacity." The expansion lets the network compute rich non-linear features before projecting back down. Empirically, 4× is a sweet spot — wider improves quality but grows params quadratically.
GELU vs ReLU
ReLU: hard zero for x < 0. Creates dead neurons — once a neuron outputs 0, its gradient is 0 and it never recovers.

GELU: smooth probabilistic curve. Small negative inputs still pass a tiny signal. No dead neurons. Better gradient flow. Now the default in all major Transformers.

Sentence-BERT bridge
BERT's token-level outputs are averaged (mean-pooled) to produce a single sentence vector. Sentence-BERT fine-tuned this with contrastive learning — making semantically similar sentences cluster together in 768-dim space.
